The Biggest Myths About Getting an Health Job — Debunked

Published: August 2025 | 8 min read

There's a lot of misinformation about Health recruitment. These myths can stop good candidates from applying or cause them to make avoidable mistakes.

Let's debunk the most common Health job myths once and for all.

Myth 1: "You Need to Know Someone to Get an Health Job"

The Truth: Health recruitment is highly regulated and transparent. Applications are scored against the person specification. Knowing someone might help you hear about opportunities, but it won't get you shortlisted if your application doesn't meet the criteria.

What Actually Matters: A well-written application that directly addresses the person specification.

Myth 2: "The Health Only Hires People with Health Experience"

The Truth: The Health actively recruits people from other sectors and countries. Transferable skills, values alignment, and relevant experience matter more than where you've worked before.

What Actually Matters: Showing how your experience (wherever it's from) meets the job requirements.

Myth 3: "If You're Qualified, You'll Get Shortlisted"

The Truth: Being qualified isn't enough. Your application must explicitly demonstrate how you meet each essential criterion. Many qualified candidates get rejected because they don't provide clear evidence.

What Actually Matters: Tailoring your application to the person specification with specific examples.

Myth 4: "Health Applications Are Just a Formality — They've Already Decided"

The Truth: Health recruitment is evidence-based and scored. Internal candidates might have an advantage in knowing the service, but they still need to score well. External candidates who provide strong evidence can and do get shortlisted.

What Actually Matters: Submitting a high-quality application regardless of whether you're internal or external.

Myth 5: "You Need a Perfect CV to Get Shortlisted"

The Truth: Your CV is important, but the supporting statement is what gets you shortlisted. The supporting statement is where you address the person specification directly.

What Actually Matters: A clear CV plus a strong, tailored supporting statement.

Myth 6: "Health Interviews Are Impossible to Prepare For"

The Truth: Health interviews are competency-based and follow predictable patterns. You can prepare by practicing STAR method answers for common Health competencies (teamwork, communication, problem-solving, values).

What Actually Matters: Preparation, practice, and having 5-7 strong STAR examples ready.

Myth 7: "International Candidates Don't Stand a Chance"

The Truth: The Health actively sponsors international candidates, especially in clinical roles. Many Trusts have dedicated international recruitment programs.

What Actually Matters: Meeting professional registration requirements and presenting your experience in Health language.

Myth 8: "You Can't Progress in the Health Without a Degree"

The Truth: While some roles require degrees, many Health careers offer progression through apprenticeships, on-the-job training, and experience. You can start as a Band 2 Healthcare Assistant and progress to Band 4+ through development programs.

What Actually Matters: Commitment to learning, strong performance, and seizing development opportunities.

Myth 9: "Health Jobs Are Secure and Easy"

The Truth: Health jobs offer stability, but they're demanding. You'll face high workloads, emotional challenges, and constant change. Job security exists, but performance and professionalism are expected.

What Actually Matters: Resilience, commitment, and genuine passion for the work.

Myth 10: "If You Don't Hear Back, You Weren't Good Enough"

The Truth: Sometimes excellent candidates don't get shortlisted because their application didn't clearly demonstrate the criteria, or because the competition was extremely high. It doesn't mean you're not good enough — it means your application needs refinement.

What Actually Matters: Learning from feedback, improving your application, and trying again.

The Real Truth About Health Recruitment

Health recruitment is fair, structured, and evidence-based. Success comes from:

  • Understanding the person specification
  • Providing clear, specific evidence
  • Demonstrating Health values
  • Preparing thoroughly for interviews
  • Being persistent and learning from rejections
